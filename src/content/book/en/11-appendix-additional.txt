    1. ㅇ and ㆁ

    In Hunminjeongeum, the initial consonant ㅇ is described as
    having a phonetic value similar to [ɦ]. In practice, however, it
    is almost devoiced, leaving only the breath sound, which
    resembles [ɦ]̥ , or is completely silent, represented as [∅] or
    [◌]. Therefore, in the translation, the pronunciation of the
    initial consonant ㅇ in Hunminjeongeum is represented as
    [◌] to visually express its meaning and presence.
    In Hunminjeongeum, when ㅇ is used as a nal consonant,
    it is silent [∅], while ㆁ as a nal consonant is pronounced
    as [ŋ].
    Starting from the 17th century, ㅇ replaced ㆁ, becoming [◌]
    in the initial position and [ŋ] in the nal position, just as it is
    pronounced in current Hangul.
    In current Hangul, the initial consonant ㅇ is the most
    fundamental letter that marks the beginning of Hangul
    consonants. Despite its presence as an independent letter,
    it has no phonetic value, making it a uniquely special
    character. Interestingly, it does not even have an of cial
    representation in the IPA (International Phonetic Alphabet)

    3. Gongwae and Bokgwae, Jasi, Chuksi, Insi

    Gongwae (䷁) and Bokgwae (䷗) are hexagrams from the I
    Ching that represent the cycles and changes of nature.
    Gongwae symbolizes darkness and the Earth. It embodies
    qualities of stillness and receptivity. It represents a state
    where everything is paused, energy is gathered, and
    preparations are made for the next change. In contrast,
    Bokgwae signi es the end of darkness and the coming of
    dawn. It symbolizes new beginnings and recovery. It marks
    the time when stagnation ends, life force and hope are
    renewed, illustrating the natural cycle of endings and
    beginnings.

    In the explanation of the vowel base characters ㆍ ㅡ ㅣ,
    the terms Jasi, Chuksi, and Insi are introduced. These are
    concepts from traditional timekeeping, which divides the day
    into twelve segments, re ecting the ow of nature and the
    changes in life energy.
    Jasi represents the time when ㆍ (Hanul-A) opens, from 11
    PM to 1 AM. It is the moment when one day ends, and a
    new day begins. During this period, darkness is at its
    deepest, energy is calm, and forces are condensed,
    symbolizing preparation for renewal.

    4. Fanqie method for representing Chinese pronunciation

    The Fanqie method is a traditional Chinese phonetic system
    used to represent the pronunciation of Chinese characters
    by dividing a character into its initial consonant and the rest.
    The initial sound is called the shengmu (initial), and the
    remaining sound, which includes the medial and nal
    sounds, is called the yunmu (rhyme).
    For example, the Chinese character dong, representing the
    east, is phonetically represented using the initial sound of
    the character deok, which starts with the consonant ㄷ, and
    the medial and nal sounds of the character hong, which
    ends with ㅗ and ㅇ.

             6. Vowel harmony

             Vowel harmony refers to the natural pairing of
             bright(Balkeum) medial vowels with other bright vowels and
             dark(Eodum) medial vowels with other dark vowels.
             Bright vowels such as ㅗ (o) and ㅏ (a) are associated with
             the imagery of the sky, evoking a sense of uplift and
             lightness. In contrast, dark vowels like ㅜ (u) and ㅓ (eo) are
             linked to the imagery of the earth, conveying a heavier and
             calmer feeling. For example, in words like malhanda (말한다,
             "to speak") and norara (놀아라, "to play"), bright vowels
             harmonize with other bright vowels. Meanwhile, in
             meokneunda (먹는다, "to eat") and ureora (울어라, "to cry"),
             dark vowels pair with other dark vowels.
             This principle is rooted in the Hasattang (Heaven Human
             Earth) philosophy that underlies the creation of vowels in
             Hunminjeongeum. It not only contributes to the naturalness
             of Korean pronunciation but also adds a musical quality to
             the language.

          8. The number of sounds in Hangul



          39.8 billion sounds

          The base consonants of Jeongeum are composed of ve
          individual characters, while the base vowels consist of three
          characters. These basic characters expand through
          additional strokes (Deotgeueum) and combinations
          (Eouleum).
          The core characters of Jeongeum include 17 initial
          consonants, 11 medial vowels. The initial sound, medial
          sound, and nal sound can each be combined with up to
          three characters. According to the creation principles of
          Jeongeum, this structure enables the formation of about
          39.8 billion complete syllables (온글자 Ongulja). This
          enormous number shows that Hangul is designed to capture
          every possible sound in nature.
          When calculating the number of initial sounds, we consider
          cases where a single consonant is used, where two
          consonants are combined, and where three consonants are
          combined. This gives us 17 + 17×17 + 17×17×17, resulting
          in 5,219 unique initial units. The same number applies to
           nal sounds. For medial vowels, the calculation is 11 +
          11×11 + 11×11×11, which gives 1,463 units.

    9. 123 mystery of ㅇㅁㅅ


    The Korean language existed long before Hangul was
    created. Yet, the shapes of a circle, square, and triangle,
    symbolizing Heaven, Earth, and Human, are re ected in the
    initial consonants of the words for "one," "two," and "three"
    in Hangul: ㅇ ㅁ ㅅ.
    Astonishingly, this pattern extends beyond Korean. In major
    world languages, the initial consonant shapes for the
    numbers 1, 2, and 3 align with ㅇ ㅁ ㅅ when written in
    Hangul:
    Hana (하나), One (원), Ichi (이치) → ㅇ
    Dul (둘), Two (투), Ni (니) → ㅁ
    Set (셋), Three (쓰리), San (산) → ㅅ
    This mirrors the Samjae (Three Elements) principle of
    Heaven (ㅇ), Earth (ㅁ), and Human (ㅅ) and their symbolic
    shapes: the circle, square, and triangle. If we apply the
    concept of 0, then the sequence of ㅇ, ㅁ, and ㅅ would
    correspond to 0, 1, and 2, rather than the typical 1, 2, and 3,
    as suggested in this book.

         10. The number ‘0’ and the initial sound ‘ㅇ’


         0 and ㅇ(Euing)are the starting point of numbers and letters.
         In the world of numbers, there is 0—a form that exists yet
         holds no value, carrying profound signi cance. In the world
         of letters, Hangul has the initial consonant ㅇ.
         In mathematics, 0 represents "nothingness," yet this state of
         emptiness is a groundbreaking invention that made the
         order of numerical systems possible. Likewise, in Hangul, ㅇ
         is a consonant without sound, yet it serves as the structural
         foundation for all syllables(Ongulja).
         Professor Nomura Hideki, in 『The Birth of Hangul』,
         described ㅇ as the "zero consonant" that supports the
         dynamic transformation of sound. As an initial consonant
         with no sound value, ㅇ allows the nal consonant of a
         preceding syllable to be carried over and pronounced at the
         beginning of the next.
         Although the number 0 appears to represent "nothing," it
         contains within it the power to create order, structure, and
         in nite possibilities. It is not merely a symbol but a
         philosophical leap and a civilizational innovation that
         transformed the way we think.

    12. Hangul, a universal writing system


    A feature-based writing system is a term in graphemics
    introduced by Professor Geoffrey Sampson of the University
    of Sussex in his 1985 book Writing Systems, to describe the
    unique characteristic of Hangul in which phonological
    features are directly re ected in letter shapes.
    A featural writing system visually represents the elements
    that constitute speech sounds by encoding the shapes of
    articulatory organs or the acoustic properties of sounds into
    written characters.
    Hunminjeongeum, Jeongeum, was designed so that both
    consonants and vowels were modeled on the forms of
    human speech organs and symbols of the Heaven-Human-
    Earth trinity, while variations in sound strength and quality
    were systematized through the addition or combination of
    strokes.
    For example, ㄱ was modeled after the shape of the tongue
    root blocking the throat, and by adding a stroke to ㄱ, the
    aspirated sound ㅋ was created. Owing to these featural
    characteristics, Hangul is widely regarded as the only
    writing system in the world that exhibits a scienti c and
    systematic

     endowed with the ability to generate an in nite number of
     sentences from a nite set of rules. This idea later
     developed into the theory of Universal Grammar, which
     posits that all human languages share a common deep
     structure.
     Although Chomsky’s theory does not directly address writing

    SeaHangul, that should be created based on the original
    principles of Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon while considering
    the demands of global advanced technology and modern
    needs. It is intended for all of humanity.
    Several consonants that were used in Hunminjeongeum are
    no longer present in modern Korean. Characters like ㆍ ㆆ
    ㅿ ㆁ have disappeared from standard usage. The loss of
    ㆍ, which was described as the fundamental and supreme
    vowel in Hunminjeongeum, represents a loss of the spirit of
    Hangul. The dot that symbolized the sky is now called Arae-
    a and is forgotten from everyday use.

    sound of a rooster crowing, yet despite the 11,172
    precomposed syllables available in Unicode, modern
    Hangul still struggles to distinguish sounds like V and F or R
    and L. Furthermore, the order of vowels and consonants, as
    well as the method of Hangul education, needs to be
    realigned with its original creation principles.

         Hangul is arguably the most effective and intuitive writing

         can instantly and effortlessly watch an overwhelming

         Net ix—with real-time Hangul subtitles powered by AI. In

         The development of new culture depends on how openly

         reinterpret them. With its deep historical roots and rich
         native culture, Korea continues to embrace global cultural
         elements in real time—constantly blending, evolving, and
         innovating. At the very heart of this process is Hangul. It
         sparks originality and innovation across elds and plays a
         central role in generating new cultural currents. Hallyu, the
         Korean Wave, is a global movement that shares these
         innovations with the world. And this movement is only
         gaining momentum.
         Structurally, Hangul is syllable-based, allowing for more
         concise and readable subtitles than English. Viewers can
         quickly grasp meaning through Hangul subtitles alone,
