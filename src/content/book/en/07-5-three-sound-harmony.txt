    Tone characteristics

    In native Korean words, 활 Hwal belongs to the level tone,
    while :돌 Dol belongs to the rising tone. Korean
    pronunciation consists of four tones: level tone, rising tone,
    departing tone, and checked tone. For example, ㆍ갈 Gal
    follows the departing tone, while ㆍ붇 But follows the
    checked tone.
    Adding one dot to the left of any character creates the
    departing tone, adding two dots creates the rising tone, and
    leaving it without a dot results in the level tone.
    The checked tone in Hanja closely resembles the departing
    tone, but in native Korean, the checked tone is not xed
    and sometimes resembles the level tone. Examples include
    ㆍ 긷 Gid and :녑 Nyeop. Other cases, such as ㆍ못 Mot
    and ㆍ입 Ip , show similarities to the departing tone. Words
    that end with consonants that close the nal sound, such as
    ㄱ ㄷ ㅂ ㅅ (익 읻 입 잇) ( iD iB iT), are classi ed as
    entering-tone words. Examples include 깍 KKaK, 닫 DaT, 밥
    BaP, 값 GaP, and 옷 OT.

         Tones and seasons

         The marking of dots for checked tones follows the same
         rule as level, rising, and departing tones. These tones
         metaphorically correspond to the four seasons:
         Level tone is calm and gentle, like spring, when all things
         slowly bloom.
         Rising tone is bright and energetic, like summer, when
         nature ourishes and grows dense.
         Departing tone is strong and determined, like autumn,
         when all things ripen and reach completion.
         Checked tone is fast and abrupt, like winter, when all things
         retreat and enter dormancy.

    Phonetic structure

    In the structure of Korean syllables, the initial sound is
    placed either to the left or above the medial sound.

    The sounds ㆆ and ㅇ are often interchangeable in native
    Korean words, allowing for variation in pronunciation.

    Among the 11 vowels that function as medial sounds, those
    that are round (ㆍ) or horizontal (ㅡ), heaven and earth
    vowel sounds, are written below the initial sound, while
    those that are vertical (ㅣ), human vowel sounds, are
    positioned to the right of the initial sound. The nal sound is
    placed below both the initial and medial sounds, completing
    the syllable structure.
    When writing initial and nal sounds together, they are
    arranged from top to bottom. When medial sounds are
    included in the syllable, the writing order follows a left-to-
    right sequence.

               Classi cation of four tones

               The four tones in native Korean words are distinguished by
               their characteristics. Words pronounced with a level or even
               tone include 활 Hwal, while those with a rising tone include
               :돌 Dol. The departing tone can be found in words like 칼
               Kal, and the checked or falling tone is represented in words
               such as 붇 But. Other words follow the same tonal
               classi cation system.


               Marking tones with dots

               In writing, these four tones can be visually distinguished by
               placing dots on the left side of the character.
               A single dot marks a departing tone. Two dots indicate a
               rising tone. If no dot is present, the word follows a level tone
               .
               The checked tone in native Korean words is not strictly
                   xed, but it is still marked with a dot when necessary. In
               Hanja pronunciation, the checked tone closely resembles
               the departing tone in its phonetic characteristics.
