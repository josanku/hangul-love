    3. Obsolete tones in sound

    In modern Hangul, the concept of tone (pitch accent) is no
    longer used. Therefore, the examples of Hangul presented
    in the Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon also omit tone
    markings. Although tone has disappeared in modern
    Hangul, there are still homophones whose meanings
    change depending on the length of the sound. For example,
    the word ъ╡┤ gul, when pronounced with a long sound, means
    "cave," but when pronounced with a short sound, it means
    "oyster." Similarly, ы░д bam, when pronounced with a long
    sound, refers to "night (time)," while with a short sound, it
    means "chestnut (nut)." Since the length of the sound is not
    marked in the writing, the meaning must be inferred from
    the context and situation.

    Root letter, basic letter, composite or combined letter

    These terms are used to classify the individual letters of
    Jeongeum. The initial sounds уЕЗ уЕЕ уЕБ уД┤ уД▒ (eu Seu Meu
    Neu Geu) and the medial sounds уЖН уЕб уЕг (Hanul-A [╩М], eu,
    i) are called root letters. From these root letters, the twelve
    basic consonants and eight basic vowels are formed
    through additional strokes and combinations representing
    the harmony of Heaven, Human, and Earth. Including the
    root characters, a total of 28 basic letters are completed.
    From these basic letters, additional strokes or combinations
    are added beside or below the base to create composite or
    combined letters such as уД▓, уЕ╕, and уЕШ. These individual
    letters (phonemes) then combine as initial consonants,
    medial vowels, or nal consonants to form whole characters
    (syllables) that produce whole sounds.

    6. Order of consonant root letters: уЕЗ уЕЕ уЕБ уД┤ уД▒

    The principle of translation is to adhere as closely as
    possible to the original content of Jeongeum. However, for
    better understanding, interpretative translation and
    additional explanations have been included.
    In Jeongeum, the order of initial sounds follows the principle
    of Daum as уД▒ уД┤ уЕБ уЕЕ уЕЗ. King Sejong, with profound
    understanding of East Asian phonology and traditional
    philosophy, created the world's most advanced writing
    system based on this deep knowledge.
    For the explanation of initial sounds, an inverted order of the
    traditional sequence re ecting уЕЗ уЕЕ уЕБ is suggested. This
    order aligns with the Hatangsa principle for vowels and is
    more intuitive for learning and self-discovery of Hangul.
    Therefore, the proposed order based on Daum is уЕЗ уЕЕ уЕБ
    уД┤ уД▒, and this sequence is applied consistently throughout
    the explanations.

         7. Philosophy terms in Korean

         Hasattang Balk-I-Eo Daum( or Hattangsa Eo-I-Balk Daum)
         is a simpli ed and intuitive Korean expression for the core
         principles of Samjae (Three Element Philosophy) and Yin-
         Yang and the Five Elements, which are fundamental to the
         creation of Hunminjeongeum. Although Yin-Yang and the
         Five Elements are key concepts in Eastern philosophy that
         explain the principles of the universe and nature, their
         Chinese-based terminology can create linguistic barriers for
         modern generations, making them seem dif cult and
         unfamiliar.
         Moreover, the Samjae (Heaven, Earth, and Human)
         philosophy is not only a central cosmological view for the
         Korean people but also the most essential concept in
         Hunminjeongeum. Therefore, expressing it in native Korean
         terms became even more necessary.
         Language is the heart of the people. It is the vessel that
         holds our spirit, culture, and history. Thought is formed
         through language, and if the language is dif cult or
         unfamiliar, the depth of that thought becomes limited. For
         younger generations who are not familiar with Chinese
         characters, terms like Samjae, Cheonjiin (Heaven, Earth,
         Human), and
         274

    fi
я┐╝
    Ohaeng (the Five Elements) can become obstacles even
    before the concepts are understood, simply because of the
    Chinese characters themselves.
    These concepts are part of our long-standing philosophical
    heritage. However, just because the terminology is in
    Chinese characters, it often creates the false perception that
    its origins are Chinese. The term Yin-Yang and the Five
    Elements can also evoke stereotypes related to fortune-
    telling, numerology, divination, or shamanism, which can
    obstruct a deeper understanding of the spirit of
    Hunminjeongeum. Therefore, it is necessary to translate
    these key ideas into Korean and reinterpret them for
    modern understanding.
    Hasattang Balk-I-Eo Daum is a Korean expression intended
    to reduce the linguistic burden and enable a more familiar
    and accurate understanding of the cosmic principles
    embraced in Hunminjeongeum.
