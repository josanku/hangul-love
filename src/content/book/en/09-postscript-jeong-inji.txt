         Origin of letters in Nature

         Wherever there are natural sounds in Heaven and Harth,
         there will inevitably be corresponding letters to represent
         them.
         In ancient times, people created letters based on these
         natural sounds, enabling them to effectively convey all
         meanings in the world. These letters were designed
         according to Hattangsa, the Three Principle (Heaven, Earth,
         and Human), making it dif cult for future generations to
         alter them carelessly.

         In uence of geography on language
         However, when the geography and environment differ, the
         sounds and energy of each region also vary.
         This leads to differences in pronunciation and language,
         requiring a writing system that re ects the unique
         characteristics of each culture and region.

         Need for a native script

         In East Asia, many countries outside of China do not have
         letters that accurately correspond to their native sounds. As
         a result, they have been forced to borrow Hanja, which are
         not suited to their linguistic needs.

         This is akin to tting a square peg into a round hole, making
         accurate communication dif cult. Each nation should have
         its own writing system that matches the unique
         characteristics of its language sounds. It is unreasonable to
         force all cultures to use the same set of letters, as doing so
         disregards the natural diversity of human language.

         Our nation is no less cultured or re ned than China in terms
         of etiquette and cultural standards. However, our language
         is fundamentally different from Chinese. Despite this, we
         lack a writing system that properly matches our spoken
         language. This forces us to use Hanja, which makes it
         dif cult to learn and understand the meaning of texts.
         For government of cials, this poses a serious challenge, as
         they struggle to fully understand local situations and
         effectively govern.

    Limitations of Idu

    In ancient times, Seol Chong of Silla Dynasty(BC 57 ~ AD
    935) created Idu, a writing system that borrowed Hanja to
    represent native Korean words. Since then, it has been
    used in both government of ces and civil society to express
    the Korean language.
    However, because Idu relies on Hanja, it is often awkward
    and cumbersome to use. The system is overly complicated
    and lacks a clear structure, leading to frequent
    miscommunication in practical language use.
    This complexity makes it dif cult for people to express
    ideas smoothly or understand written texts with ease.
    Idu served a temporary purpose but ultimately failed to fully
    accommodate the unique sounds and syntax of Korean.
    This revealed the limitations of borrowing foreign characters
    to represent a native language.

         Divine creation

         In December 1443, during the winter, King Sejong the Great
         invented 28 letters and named them Hunminjeongeum (The
         Right Sounds for the People).
         To help people understand and use the new letters, he
         presented simple examples and explanations.

         Design principles: Balk-I-Eo, Hattangsa, Daum
         The shapes of the letters were inspired by ancient
         characters, but their sounds were harmoniously arranged
         according to the seven musical notes.
         They were designed to re ect the principle of
         Hattangsa(Heaven, Earth, and Human) as well as the
         harmony of Balk-I-Eo(Yin-Yang), making the system both
         mystical and perfect.

         28 letters, in nite sounds
         With just 28 basic letters, an in nite number of
         combinations can be created, enabling:
         Conciseness while clearly conveying the main point.
         Precision in meaning while allowing for widespread
         communication.

         Easy and practical

         With Jeongeum, a wise person can master it in just half a
         day, while even those who learn slowly can become
         pro cient within ten days. Using this writing system, one
         can interpret Hanja texts and understand their meanings
         clearly. It is also useful for recording legal documents and
         court cases, allowing readers to fully grasp the details and
         context. This makes Hangul not only easy to learn but also
         highly practical in everyday life and of cial matters.

    Like music and melody, the sounds harmonize

    Contributors

    At last, King Sejong the Great instructed that detailed
    explanations be added to help people easily understand the
    new writing system. He wanted the knowledge to be
    accessible to everyone, ensuring that even those without
    formal education could learn on their own. In response to
    this royal command, Jeong Inji humbly collaborated with:
    Choi Hang, a scholar of the Jiphyeonjeon (Hall of
    Worthies), Park Paengnyeon, Shin Suk-ju, and Seong
    Sammun, who were also esteemed scholars and of cials,
    Kang Huian, a government of cial, Yi Gae and Yi Seonro,
    assistant scholars at the Jiphyeonjeon.


    Accessible by all people

    Together, they compiled explanations and examples to
    illustrate the usage of Hangul, presenting them in a clear
    and concise manner. Their work aimed to make
    Hunminjeongeum accessible and understandable to all
    people, regardless of their educational background.
